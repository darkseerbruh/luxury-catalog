## TITLE
How to authenticate an Hermès bag: the markers worth checking

## EXCERPT
A field guide to the Hermès details counterfeits get wrong, why even a flawless-looking bag is never proof, and why this brand more than any other belongs in front of a real authenticator.

## BODY

Hermès is the brand where the usual advice stops being enough. The bags are made by hand, the resale stakes are high, and the counterfeits aimed at them are the best in the world. A skilled Hermès fake, often called a "superfake," can pass nearly every visual check a careful buyer can run, in photos and sometimes even in the hand. So this guide teaches the markers worth knowing, then says plainly what the others only imply: on Hermès, looking right gets you less than it does anywhere else.

A genuine bag carries a consistent set of details:

- a blind stamp pressed into the leather
- clean hardware engraving on solid metal
- hand saddle-stitched seams
- proportions that match the size exactly

> Checking these markers tells you when something is wrong. It can never tell you, on its own, that a bag is genuine, and on Hermès that gap is at its widest, because the best counterfeits are built specifically to pass these looks. Treat what follows as a way to catch the weaker fakes and narrow your doubt, never a verdict. Here more than anywhere, the only real confirmation is a qualified human who can hold the bag.

The sections below go deeper on each marker in the diagram above: what to look for, why it works, and where it stops being reliable, which on this brand is sooner than you might think.

## The blind stamp

[diagram: hermes-visual-blindstamp]

Hermès presses a small mark into the leather without ink, which is why it is called a blind stamp. It records the craftsman and the year of production, and on exotic-skin bags it also carries a code for the skin. On a genuine bag the impression is clean and evenly set, with crisp characters sunk into the hide. Placement is era-dependent, and this matters: since roughly 2016 Hermès has set the stamp inside the bag, on the left near the back of the front flap, while older bags carry it in other spots. So an unfamiliar location is not automatically a flag on a vintage bag, and you cannot treat one fixed position as the rule across all eras. Read the impression for the obvious mistakes, characters that are uneven, shallow, or sloppily struck, but know that counterfeiters reproduce the stamp closely. A wrong-looking stamp is a reason to walk; a right-looking one clears one narrow hurdle and nothing more.

## Hardware and engraving

[diagram: hermes-visual-hardware]

Hermès hardware is solid and substantial, and it has real weight in the hand rather than feeling light or hollow. The engraving, typically HERMÈS PARIS over MADE IN FRANCE, is clean, sharp, and evenly cut, with consistent letter spacing and depth. Counterfeits can betray themselves here with engraving that reads shallow, smudged, or unevenly spaced, or with hardware that feels cheap and light. Treat a clear hardware flaw as a strong reason to pass. But hold the caveat firmly: the best fakes get the hardware convincingly right, including the weight, so clean, heavy hardware is a supporting signal, never confirmation.

## Saddle stitching

[diagram: hermes-visual-saddlestitch]

This is the marker that leans hardest on the human hand, and it is the one a machine struggles most to fake. Hermès bags are sewn with a saddle stitch done by hand, two needles working a single thread from both sides of the seam. Because it is human work, the result is strong and even but deliberately and very slightly irregular, with tiny natural variations no two seams share exactly. A machine produces stitches that are too perfectly uniform, and a counterfeit that imitates the hand-stitched look usually cannot reproduce its precise, slightly-irregular character at scale. This is among the most telling markers on the bag, which is exactly why it is so hard to read from a photo and why a real authenticator examines it closely in person. Uniform, mechanical, or sloppy seams are a reason to pass; convincing hand stitching is encouraging, not conclusive.

## Proportions and shape

[diagram: hermes-visual-proportions]

Each Hermès style and size has an exact, documented shape, and the brand's construction holds those proportions precisely. The body should sit and hold its form correctly for its size, with handle length, base, and flap all in the right relationship. Counterfeits frequently miss subtly here, with a silhouette that sags, sits a touch too tall or too wide, or carries handles slightly out of proportion. The trouble is that these are fine differences, hard to judge without a genuine reference of the same size beside you, and the best fakes get the shape close. So an obviously-off silhouette is a flag, while a correct-looking one is only one more box checked, not an answer.

## The honest bottom line
Lay the checks side by side and the logic is lopsided, and on Hermès it is lopsided more than anywhere else we cover. A marker that is clearly wrong, a sloppily struck blind stamp, light or shallow-engraved hardware, mechanical stitching, an off silhouette, is a sound reason to pass. But a bag that clears every check has earned only the narrowest statement of all our guides: nothing visible is wrong. On most brands that already falls short of proof. On Hermès, where the counterfeits are built precisely to pass every one of these looks, it falls shorter still. Consistent markers mean less here than on any brand in this slate.

> We do not authenticate bags and we do not guarantee authenticity. On Hermès this is not a formality, it is the heart of the matter: a bag that is consistent across every marker here is consistent, not confirmed, and the best fakes are made to be consistent. Before you buy, sell, or insure an Hermès bag, put it in the hands of a qualified professional authenticator who can examine it in person. On this brand more than any other, that is the only step that confirms it.

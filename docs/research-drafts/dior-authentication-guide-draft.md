## TITLE
How to authenticate a Dior bag: the markers worth checking

## EXCERPT
A field guide to the Dior details counterfeits get wrong, why a passing check is never proof, and when to put a bag in front of a real authenticator.

## BODY

Dior, and the Lady Dior in particular, is one of the most replicated single handbags on the market, which means there are plenty of convincing fakes and a well-known set of details worth checking. A genuine bag leaves a consistent trail:

- the charm tag and lettering
- a date code that matches the Made in stamp
- even, taut cannage stitching
- weighty, cleanly cast hardware and letter charms

> Checking these markers tells you when something is wrong. It can never tell you, on its own, that a bag is genuine, because a good counterfeit copies the visible details. Treat what follows as a way to catch fakes and narrow your doubt, not a verdict. When the answer truly matters, the only real confirmation is a person who can hold the bag.

The sections below go deeper on each marker in the diagram above: what to look for, why it works, and where it stops being reliable.

## The charm tag

[diagram: dior-visual-charmtag]

On the Lady Dior, the bag's signature charms spell out D-I-O-R, and one of them, the "O," carries a small leather tag. On a genuine bag the back of that tag reads exactly "Christian Dior," nothing more and nothing less. This is a precise, checkable detail, and it is one counterfeits get wrong in revealing ways: extra wording, missing words, a different font, characters that are unevenly spaced, or text that reads shallow and printed rather than cleanly stamped. Read it carefully against what a real tag says. A tag that gets the wording or the lettering wrong is a strong reason to walk away. A tag that reads correctly clears that one hurdle, which is useful but not proof, since the wording itself is widely known and copied.

## The date code and Made in stamp

[diagram: dior-visual-datecode]

Genuine Dior bags carry a date code, often on a small interior tab, alongside a Made in stamp naming the country of manufacture. The single most useful check here is consistency between the two: the date code and the Made in stamp should agree, and a code whose format or origin does not square with the stamp is a documented mismatch. That kind of inconsistency is one of the few signals strong enough to lean on, a defensible reason to call a bag likely not authentic. One caution keeps this honest: the date-code format has changed across production eras, so a code that looks unfamiliar is not automatically wrong, and you have to read it against the right period rather than one fixed template. So a clear mismatch between code and stamp is a real red flag, while a code that simply looks plausible, on its own, proves nothing, because counterfeiters copy code formats too.

## Cannage stitching

[diagram: dior-visual-cannage]

Cannage is Dior's signature quilted pattern, the raised, padded diamond or rectangular grid stitched across the body of the bag, named after the caning on classic Napoleon III chairs. Construction quality shows here first. On a genuine bag the cannage is even and taut, the rows are symmetrical, and each padded section has a consistent, controlled puffiness across the whole panel. Counterfeits tend to fall short with stitching that looks flat and underfilled or, conversely, sloppy and uneven, with rows that drift out of alignment or puffiness that varies from one section to the next. Authentic bags are not flawless, and an older one will show honest wear, so you are not hunting for perfection. You are watching for the mistakes a careful maker would not ship. Flat, loose, or uneven cannage is one of the clearer places a fake reveals itself.

## Hardware and letter charms

[diagram: dior-visual-hardware]

The hardware and the D-I-O-R charms are a useful tactile check. On a genuine bag the metal is weighty and solid rather than light or hollow, the casting is clean and sharp with no rough edges or bubbling, and the four letter charms are evenly spaced and consistent in finish. Counterfeits often miss here with charms that feel cheap and light, lettering that is poorly cast or slightly misshapen, uneven spacing between the letters, or plating that looks thin. Treat a clear hardware flaw as a strong reason to pass, and treat clean, weighty hardware as one supporting signal among several, never a verdict on its own.

## The honest bottom line
Lay the checks side by side and the logic is lopsided on purpose. A marker that is clearly wrong, a charm tag with the wrong wording, a date code that contradicts the Made in stamp, flat or uneven cannage, light and roughly cast charms, is a sound reason to pass. A bag that clears every check has earned only the narrower statement: nothing visible is wrong, which is not the same as proven genuine, because a capable fake is built to pass exactly these looks. That gap matters especially on the Lady Dior, one of the most heavily replicated bags there is.

> We do not authenticate bags and we do not guarantee authenticity. A bag that is consistent across every marker here is consistent, not confirmed. When real money is on the line, before you buy something expensive, or sell or insure it, put the bag in the hands of a professional authenticator who can examine it in person. That is the only step that confirms it.

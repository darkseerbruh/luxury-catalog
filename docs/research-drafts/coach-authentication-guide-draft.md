## TITLE
How to authenticate a Coach bag: the markers worth checking

## EXCERPT
A field guide to the Coach details counterfeits get wrong, why a passing check is never proof, and when to put a bag in front of a real authenticator.

## BODY (exactly as seeded — uses the renderer's markup subset + the diagram token)

Coach is widely counterfeited, which also makes it one of the most teachable brands to check. The bags leave a clear trail:

- a stamped leather creed
- a serial number
- consistent, even stitching
- a printed signature canvas

> Checking these markers tells you when something is wrong. It can never tell you, on its own, that a bag is genuine, because a good counterfeit copies the visible details. Treat what follows as a way to catch fakes and narrow your doubt, not a verdict. When the answer truly matters, the only real confirmation is a person who can hold the bag.

## The creed patch
The creed is the small leather tag sewn inside the bag, carrying a few lines about Coach and, on most bags, the serial or style number. Placement varies by style and era. It can sit in the lining seam or on an inner wall, so an unexpected spot is not a red flag on its own. On a genuine piece the lettering is heat-stamped, pressed into the leather, with clean, centered, evenly spaced text. A fake often gets the stamping wrong: flat printed or inked text, a plastic or fabric patch instead of leather, or crooked, blurry letters. Some small styles never carried a creed at all, so a missing patch is not automatically a fake.

## The serial number
On most bags made after the 1970s the serial or style number sits on the creed, and for a short window around 2014 to 2016 Coach moved the style number to a small white tag tucked into an inner-pocket corner. A genuine number is stamped into the leather, never printed, and runs to more than five digits. A serial that begins with the letters N through Z is almost always fake. The catch most guides skip: a correct-looking serial proves nothing, because counterfeiters copy real numbers. A wrong one is a strong red flag, but a right one is not a sign of a real bag.

## Stitching
Look at the stitching along the seams, the handles, and around the creed. Coach stitching is even and straight, with steady spacing. It is not flawless, and a little wear on an older bag is normal, so you are watching for the obvious tells: crooked or overlapping stitches, loose or fraying threads, or thread that changes color or thickness partway along a seam.

## Signature canvas
Signature canvas is Coach's coated canvas printed with the repeating C pattern, instead of plain leather. On the front, the pattern looks balanced and centered, and the print is crisp. It does not need to line up along the side and bottom seams, and a few styles are exceptions, so a seam that does not match is normal. What is worth a second look is print that is blurry or uneven, or a front that looks clearly off-center.

## Materials and feel
This one is hard to judge from a photo. In your hands it tells you more. Real glove-tanned Coach leather feels soft and substantial, with a natural grain you can see: small pores, light creases, and slight variation, because it is left natural and aniline-dyed. It smells like leather, and older bags soften and develop a patina. Fakes tend to feel stiff and plastic and can smell chemical. One honest caveat: some newer Coach bags use coated or thinner leather, so a stiffer feel alone is not proof of a fake.

## The zipper myth
The brand name stamped on the zipper pull tells you nothing. Coach has used many different zipper makers over the years, and a counterfeiter can fit the same well-known ones, like YKK, which is just a zipper manufacturer. Authenticate the bag, not the zipper.

## The honest bottom line
If a marker is clearly wrong, a flat-printed creed, a serial that does not fit, a front pattern that looks off, you have good reason to walk away. If every marker looks right, the most you can truthfully say is that nothing looks wrong, which is not the same as genuine.

> We do not authenticate bags and we do not guarantee authenticity. When the stakes are real, before you buy something expensive, or sell or insure it, put the bag in front of a professional authenticator who can examine it in hand. That is the step that actually confirms it.

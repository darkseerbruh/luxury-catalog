## TITLE
How to authenticate a Coach bag: the markers worth checking

## EXCERPT
A field guide to the Coach details counterfeits get wrong, why a passing check is never proof, and when to put a bag in front of a real authenticator.

## BODY (exactly as it will be seeded — uses the renderer's markup subset)

Coach is widely counterfeited, which also makes it one of the most teachable brands to check. The bags leave a clear trail:

- a stamped leather creed
- a serial number
- consistent, even stitching
- a printed signature canvas

> Checking these markers tells you when something is wrong. It can never tell you, on its own, that a bag is genuine, because a good counterfeit copies the visible details. Treat what follows as a way to catch fakes and narrow your doubt, not a verdict. When the answer truly matters, the only real confirmation is a person who can hold the bag.

## 1. The creed patch
The creed is the small leather patch sewn inside the bag, carrying a few lines about Coach and, on most bags, the serial or style number. On a genuine piece the text is heat-stamped into the leather, pressed in and slightly debossed, with clean, centered, evenly spaced lettering and correct spelling. Fashionphile, which authenticates Coach professionally, treats this stamping as a core check. What a fake tends to get wrong is the stamping itself: text that is printed or inked on rather than pressed in, a patch made of plastic or fabric instead of leather, crooked or blurry letters, or a spelling or spacing slip. One thing that is not a red flag: some small styles, like clutches, minis, and swing packs, never carried a creed at all, so a missing patch is not automatically a fake.

## 2. The serial or style number
On most bags made after the 1970s the number lives on the creed patch, and for a short window around 2014 to 2016 Coach moved the style number to a separate white fabric tag sewn into a seam. Like the creed, a genuine number is embossed into the leather, never printed or painted on. A few specifics that reputable Coach authenticators and the PurseForum community agree on: the number runs to more than five digits, and a serial that begins with the letters N through Z is almost always fake. Here is the part most guides skip. A correct-looking serial proves nothing, because counterfeiters copy real numbers straight off genuine bags. The serial is useful in only one direction. A wrong one is a strong sign of a fake, but a right one is not a sign of a real bag.

## 3. Stitching
Look at the stitching along the seams, the handles, and around the creed. Coach stitching is even and straight, with consistent spacing and tension. It is not flawless, and a stray imperfection on an older bag is normal, so you are looking for the obvious tells rather than perfection: crooked or overlapping stitches, loose or fraying threads, or thread that changes color or thickness partway along a seam.

## 4. The signature C canvas
If the bag is signature canvas, check how the C pattern is printed. On the front, the two halves of the pattern should mirror each other, and the print should be crisp and even. The catch, and it is a big one, is that the C's are not supposed to line up perfectly along the side and bottom seams, especially on curved shapes, because matching every seam would waste fabric. A few styles, like the Optic prints, Scarf Totes, and some Scribble Hobos, do not follow the mirror rule at all. So a seam that does not line up is normal, not a red flag on its own. Blurry or uneven printing, or a front that clearly does not mirror itself, is what is worth a second look.

## 5. Materials and hardware
Materials and hardware are a softer check, but worth a glance. Coach has historically used full-grain or glove-tanned leather and solid metal hardware with real weight to it. Hardware that feels hollow or lightweight, or trim that cracks or peels, runs against that. This one is harder to judge from a photo and easy to talk yourself into, so weigh it lightly.

## 6. The zipper myth
The zipper brand does not authenticate a Coach bag. Over the years Coach has used several zipper makers, including Talon, Eclair, RiRi, YKK, and Ziplon, so an unfamiliar brand on the pull is not a problem. And a counterfeiter can buy genuine YKK zippers as easily as anyone, so a real YKK stamp is not proof of anything either. Authenticate the bag, not the zipper.

## The honest bottom line
If a marker is clearly wrong, a misspelled creed, a printed serial, a front pattern that does not mirror, you have good reason to walk away. If every marker looks right, the most you can truthfully say is that nothing looks wrong, which is not the same as genuine.

> We do not authenticate bags and we do not guarantee authenticity. When the stakes are real, before you buy something expensive, or sell or insure it, put the bag in front of a professional authenticator who can examine it in hand. That is the step that actually confirms it.

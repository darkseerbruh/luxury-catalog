## The hook (de-gatekeeping, one paragraph)

People ask "what is my bag's color actually called," half expecting the answer to be no, that's
just marketing. It is not. Read the houses' own current websites and most luxury bag colors really
do carry a house name. What changes from house to house is the depth. A couple of houses run a deep
proprietary system where a color has a name and a code that follows the bag for life. Most houses
name their current colors plainly, real names, just without the coded machinery. And two houses are
the genuine exception: they label by leather and color family, no per-color name at all. So the
useful question is not "does it have a name," it is "how deep does this house go." Here are the
three tiers.

## How we checked

The current color names below were captured on 2026-06-28 directly from each house's own US website

## Tier 1: A deep proprietary lexicon (a name, and often a code, that follows the bag for life)

These are the two houses with a real color dictionary, where the name is the color's permanent house
identity, not a per-season description.

**Hermès.** Every Hermès color has an official name and a two-character code, and the code is the
anchor that survives across seasons and leathers. The confirmed core set includes **Noir 89** (black),
**Gold / Or 06** (warm camel), **Rouge H 46** (a deep brown-red in the house since 1925), **Etoupe
18** (the bestselling grey-brown taupe), and **Orange H / Feu 93** (the signature box orange). So
"Etoupe" is not a way of describing a taupe bag. It is the color's actual house name, and 18 is its
code. (Source: `hermes.md` section 2.)

The honest caveat to keep in the piece: not every Hermès color has a code sourced yet. The archive
fills codes for the staple neutrals (Gris Tourterelle 81, Nata I2, Mushroom 0T, Craie 10 and more,
cross-checked across reseller code charts and Christie's), but some of the newest seasonal names are
logged with the name confirmed and the code still null. State the codes that are sourced; for the
rest, say the color has a name whose code is still being sourced, rather than inventing a number.
(Source: `hermes.md` sections 2 to 4.)

**Bottega Veneta.** Bottega is the other deep-lexicon house, the counter-case to most of Italian
luxury. It names its colors richly, and several are now confirmed on bottegaveneta.com itself.
**Parakeet** (the grassy acid green Daniel Lee reintroduced for Spring/Summer 2021, now built into
the brand's store design) is double-sourced official. **Travertine** and **Fondant** were previously
reseller-attributed and are now **house-confirmed (Chrome 2026-06-28)**: Travertine appears on the
Small Barbara Tote, and Fondant sits right in the product-URL slug `mini-andiamo-fondant`. The same
pass also confirmed Mineral, Espresso, Ecru, Deep Mahogany, Butter Yellow, Dark Olive and Gray Clay
as current BV names. (Source: `bottega-veneta.md` section 3; `chrome-com-colors-2026.md` Bottega.)

The hedge that still matters: Bottega's wider list (Porridge, Barolo, Pinecone, the "Washed" pastels
and dozens more) comes from how a major reseller, Fashionphile, catalogs the colors. Those are very
likely BV's own names, but they are **reseller-attributed**, not yet confirmed on the brand site, so
the archive marks them medium confidence pending a site confirm. In the piece: Parakeet, Travertine
and Fondant are safe to call official Bottega colors; the longer list is the names collectors and
resellers use, a strong lead, not yet house-verified. (Source: `bottega-veneta.md` "Official vs
descriptive" rule.)

## Tier 2: Names its colors plainly (real names, no coded depth)

These houses give their current colors genuine names on their own sites. Read the product page and
the color has a real, often evocative name. They just do not run Hermès's coded, follows-for-life
machinery. All names below are **house-confirmed (Chrome 2026-06-28)** from each brand's own site.

- **Dior.** Dior names its colors, full names on the tile: **Rose Tendre**, **Garance Red**, **Gris
  Flanelle**, **Craie**, **Trench**, plus Khaki and Powder Beige. Garance (madder red), Craie (chalk)
  and Gris Flanelle (flannel grey) are Dior's reused house neutral lexicon. (Earlier archive notes
  filed Dior under "doesn't name its colors." That was wrong, and the Chrome capture corrects it.)
- **Celine.** The official color rides on the tile after a semicolon and in the SKU: **Miel** (honey),
  **Syrah**, **Safari**, **Bronze**, **Rice**, with two-tone canvas named as a pair (Navy / Red).
- **Saint Laurent.** YSL names its colors but hides the name behind a family word in the title. The
  real house name renders on the product page after the price: **Santal** (sandalwood), **Rouge
  Cabernet**, **Dark Oak**, confirmed on the Mombasa line.
- **Prada.** Prada names colors in specific English and maps each to a stable color code in the SKU:
  **Baltic Blue**, **Camel Brown**, **Brandy**, with the SKU's F-code (e.g. F0002 = Black) holding
  across models. Named and code-keyed, but the names are descriptive-specific rather than Hermès-poetic.
- **Loewe.** Loewe names its colors and code-keys them in the SKU tail: **Squirrel**, **Lemon**,
  **Dark Chestnut**, **Soft White**, read off the product page's Colour label and swatch list.
- **Louis Vuitton.** LV's color names live on the product page, not the listing grid: the current Epi
  line carries **Rose Miami**, **Ocean Blue** and **Orange California**, confirmed across the Alma BB
  and Speedy Soft 30.

(Source for all of Tier 2: `chrome-com-colors-2026.md`, the Dior, Celine, Saint Laurent, Prada,

## Tier 3: Family or descriptor only (the genuine no-name houses)

Two houses really do leave the color unnamed. Read their own sites and the "color" is a leather-plus-
family descriptor, not a per-color house name.

- **Gucci.** Every signal is a plain descriptor. The color filter is family buckets only (Beige,
  Black, Brown and so on), and the product page labels the variant as material plus family, for
  example **"black GG leather"** or **"sand and brown GG canvas."** The "Color: Gold" on the page is
  hardware, not a colorway. Gucci's identifying layer is the model and the canvas (GG Supreme, GG
  canvas), not color.
- **Fendi.** Same camp. The filter is families only, and titles read as descriptor plus material, for
  example **"Fendigraphy Mini, Light blue"** or a "Hazelnut" raffia. "Light blue" and "Hazelnut" are
  descriptions, not a coded lexicon. Fendi's weight sits on the model and material (Peekaboo,
  Baguette; FF denim, raffia, selleria), not color.

(Source: `chrome-com-colors-2026.md`, the Gucci and Fendi sections, captured 2026-06-28. The absence
of a named-color layer is itself the captured fact, high-confidence and house-published.)

## The special case: Chanel names neither

Chanel sits outside the three tiers because it runs a different system entirely. A Chanel bag carries
a **style code** and a **season code** (like 18C), and the color itself is left unnamed. So "what
pink was Cruise 2018" has no official Chanel color name to give, even though the season code is a
real, decodable Chanel fact in its own right. That date-and-season code is its own story, and a good
companion to this one. (Source: `chanel.md` via the cross-house context in `bottega-veneta.md`;
content-ideas.md, the Chanel date-code decoder angle.)

## Why this answers a real question

This is why "what is my bag's color called" lands differently from house to house. For an Hermès bag
the color has a house name and usually a code, so the answer is exact and permanent. For a Bottega,
Parakeet, Travertine and Fondant are confirmed house names and the rest are the names the resale
market uses. For a Dior, Celine, Saint Laurent, Prada, Loewe or Louis Vuitton bag, there is a real
current name to look up, just without the coded depth. For a Gucci or Fendi bag, the honest answer is
that there is no per-color house name: the bag is identified by leather, family and model. And for a
Chanel bag, the name to decode is the season code, not the color. Knowing which house you are holding
tells you which kind of answer to expect.

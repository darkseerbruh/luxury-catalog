# The Hermès leather guide: Togo, Clemence, Epsom, Swift, and how each one behaves

*Draft. Reference article (GEO, decision-aid). Built from `docs/research-drafts/seasonal-archive/hermes.md` §1. Not yet wired into the live Articles system; that is the Content lane's owner-gated seeding step.*

The leather is half the bag at Hermès, and the names get treated like a secret handshake. They are not. Each leather has an official name, a grain you can learn to recognize, and a way it behaves over time, and once you know the handful that matter you can read a listing properly. Togo holds its shape, Clemence slouches softer and heavier, Epsom stays rigid and keeps its color, Swift takes bright color beautifully but scratches. This is the working vocabulary, one tight identity per leather: grain, behavior, and whether it is a current core leather or a heritage one you will mostly see on vintage and special-order pieces. Read it as a decision aid for what suits how you carry, not a claim about what any leather is worth.

One framing note. The official Hermès name is "Veau X" (calf X) for calf leathers, "Chèvre X" for goat, "Vache X" for cowhide. "Heritage" is the trade tag for legacy or limited leathers that are still produced but show up mostly on vintage, limited, or special-order bags. Status reflects the leather groupings as of the 2024 source update; Hermès rotates leathers in and out, so read status as current as of 2024 unless noted.

## The four you will see most

These are the workhorses of the modern lineup, and the comparison people actually search for.

| Leather | Official name | Grain and behavior | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Togo | Veau Togo | Fine pebbled grain, female baby calf; scratch-resistant, light, holds its shape despite a slightly slouchy look. Introduced 1997. | Current core |
| Clemence | Veau Taurillon Clemence | Larger, flatter, softer grain from male bull calf; semi-matte, heavier, a relaxed slouch. Often called Togo's "half-sister." | Current core |
| Epsom | Veau Epsom | Heat-embossed (printed) fine even grain; rigid, lightweight, scratch-resistant, holds its shape and takes color brightly. Introduced 2004 as Courchevel's replacement. | Current core |
| Swift | Veau Swift (formerly Gulliver) | Soft matte, very light grain, near-smooth; takes bright color superbly and reflects light, but scratches easily. Gulliver discontinued 1999; Swift introduced 2004/2005. | Current core |

The quick decision read: Togo for a structured-but-forgiving everyday calf, Clemence if you like a softer, heavier slouch, Epsom if you want rigid structure and the cleanest color (it is the embossed one, so it is the most uniform), and Swift if you are chasing a rich saturated color and do not mind babying it against scratches.

## The heritage leathers (still made, mostly vintage and special-order)

These are the legacy leathers. They are still produced but you will predominantly see them on vintage, limited, or special-order pieces, and several are sought-after for exactly that reason.

| Leather | Official name | Grain and behavior | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Box (Box Calf) | Veau Box / Boxcalf | The oldest Hermès leather, named for shoemaker Joseph Box; smooth, glossy, rigid, scratches easily then patinates to a mirror sheen. Mostly vintage and neutral now. | Heritage (still produced) |
| Barenia | Barenia (calfskin) | Smooth calfskin with a slight sheen, water- and scratch-resistant (oil-absorbing); originally for saddles, first on bags in the 1970s. Develops patina and darkens. | Heritage (sought-after) |
| Barenia Faubourg | Barenia Faubourg | A reinterpretation of Barenia calfskin; similar relaxed look, patinas and darkens, more supple than Barenia. Introduced 2016. | Heritage (modern) |
| Tadelakt | Veau Tadelakt | Smooth, soft but structured (Box-like); can look faintly mottled or streaked, may blister when wet. Showcases color. Introduced 2007, small goods. | Heritage (still produced) |
| Doblis | Veau Doblis | Hermès' suede; very soft, cannot get wet, shines where handled. First on bags in the 1930s, limited to small bags and clutches. | Heritage (intermittent) |
| Chamonix | Veau Chamonix | Smooth matte calf, similar to Box but without the high gloss; vintage and limited. | Heritage |
| Vache Naturel | Vache Naturel(le) | Untreated smooth leather, very delicate; stains, scratches, and water-spots, then darkens and patinas beautifully. | Heritage (returned) |

Useful for reading older listings, even though you will not see these on new bags.

|---|---|---|---|
| Chèvre de Coromandel | Chèvre de Coromandel | Goat; slightly larger grain and a more pronounced spine than Mysore. The predecessor to Mysore. | Discontinued (replaced by Mysore) |

## The exotics

The exotic skins, with the stamp struck next to the blind stamp where it is documented. A note on those stamp symbols: they come from the established authentication beat and are widely documented, but they are not double-confirmed here, so treat them as markers to check rather than a settled fact.

| Skin | Official name | Identity | Stamp |
|---|---|---|---|
| Niloticus Crocodile | Crocodile Niloticus | Nile croc; smaller, more uniform rectangular scales than Porosus; matte or shiny (lisse). | ∷ (two dots) |
| Porosus Crocodile | Crocodile Porosus | Saltwater croc; the smallest, most prized scales; sourced from Australia. | ^ (caret) |
| Alligator | Alligator Mississippiensis | Larger, squarer scales with a visible umbilical / follicle line; US-sourced; matte or shiny. | □ (square) |
| Ostrich | Autruche | Quill-bump grain; takes color richly. Cycles in and out of production. | — |
| Lizard | Lézard (Niloticus / Varanus) | Tiny fine scales with a high natural shine; used on small bags like the Mini Kelly and Constance. Cyclical. | — |

## How to use this

This is a decision aid, not a value claim. The right leather is the one that matches how you carry: structured or slouchy, low-maintenance or color-forward, current-production or a heritage piece you are willing to baby. Nothing here speaks to price or how a leather holds value; that is a separate question with its own data. And the exotic stamp symbols are markers to check against a professional authentication, not a verdict you should make on your own from a photo.

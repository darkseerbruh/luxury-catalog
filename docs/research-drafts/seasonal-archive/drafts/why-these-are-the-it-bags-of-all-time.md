# Why are these the it bags of all time?

Ask ten people for the greatest handbag ever made and you will not get a list. You
will get an argument. That is the fun of it, and it is also the point of this page.

Here is the tension we kept running into, and the one that organizes everything below.
A bag earns "it bag of all time" on two axes at once, and they pull in different
directions. One is **heritage and mythology**: who designed it, who carried it, the
story that gets told and retold until the bag means something before you have even
picked it up. The other is **real-world recognition**: the bag your coworker, your
mom, and a stranger on the train all clock from across the room, because it is
genuinely everywhere.

Most bags are strong on one axis. The all-timers are strong on both, and that is why a
five-figure Birkin and a Neverfull that costs a fraction of it sit on the same list
without contradiction. The Birkin is myth made physical, and you still see it in the
wild. The Neverfull has almost no myth, and it is one of the most recognized bags on
earth. Different routes, same destination: a bag the culture agreed on.

A note on how to read this, because we mean it. **Past the top two, this is our canon,
not a decree.** Taste is subjective, and the ranking below reflects a point of view,
not a measurement. We are confident about the consensus six. We get more opinionated
the further down we go, and by the back stretch we are openly debating with ourselves.
Disagree freely. That is the right response to a ranking like this.

One more thing we will not do: we are not telling you any of these is a good
"investment," and we are not quoting prices here. Resale values move, and the dated,
sourced ranges live on each bag's own page where we can keep them honest. When we
mention value below, treat it as a general read, not an appraisal.

## The locked six

These are the bags we do not argue about. Every one clears both axes by a wide margin:
a real origin story *and* the kind of recognition that needs no caption.

### 1. Hermès Birkin

The Birkin is the bag every other "it bag" gets measured against. The mythology is
unusually thick, even by luxury standards, and it is built on real scarcity: Hermès
makes them in limited numbers, the leathers and hardware are chosen and combined with
intent, and the wait to buy one at retail is itself part of the legend. The widely
told origin is that it was created for and named after the actor Jane Birkin. We are
keeping the specific founding year off this page until we can cite it cleanly from our
own archive, because a wrong date is worse than no date. What is not in dispute is the
result: across leathers like Togo and Epsom (both documented in our Hermès leather
guide), the Birkin is the bag that holds its cultural weight better than almost
anything made.

### 2. Hermès Kelly

The Kelly is the Birkin's older, more formal sibling, and for a lot of people it is the
more beautiful of the two. The single trapezoidal flap, the turn-lock, the optional
shoulder strap: it reads as a bag with somewhere to be. Its fame is tied to a midcentury
film star whose name it carries, the kind of association that turns an object into
shorthand. As with the Birkin, we are holding the exact origin year for a clean source
rather than asserting it. What earns its rank is plain enough without the date: decades
on, the Kelly is still the bag that signals quiet, knowing taste, and the Sellier
(structured) versions in particular are some of the most recognizable silhouettes in
the room.

### 3. Chanel Classic Flap

If the Hermès pair is about scarcity, the Classic Flap is about ubiquity done right: the
quilted leather, the chain-and-leather strap, the interlocking-CC turn-lock. It is one
of the most copied bags ever made, which is its own kind of proof. A useful, real tell
while we are here: a genuine Classic Flap has one interior zip pocket. Two means a fake,
or a much older bag. (For dating a Chanel properly, our date-code decoder walks the
serial series, the season code, and the 2021 microchip cutover.) The Flap earns its spot
because it is both deeply storied and instantly legible, the bag that taught a couple of
generations what "a Chanel" looks like.

### 4. Louis Vuitton Speedy

The Speedy is the people's icon, and its origin is one of the best stories in the
canon. It descends from Louis Vuitton's 1930 "Express," and the Speedy 25 was born in
1965 from an Audrey Hepburn request to shrink the Keepall travel duffle down to a city
size (sourced to Vogue's LV history and Yoogi's Closet). That is the whole appeal in
one fact: a travel bag, miniaturized for real life, that never stopped working. It is
approachable, it is everywhere, and it has been everywhere for decades. Recognition
without trying.

### 5. Lady Dior

The Lady Dior is the rare bag whose myth and name arrived together. By the accounts we
have, it was created in the mid-1990s (commonly cited as 1995, a date we are treating as
likely-but-unverified until our own archive confirms it), originally called the
"Chouchou," and was famously associated with Diana, Princess of Wales, after which it
was renamed in her honor. The cannage quilting and the dangling D-I-O-R charms are
unmistakable. It carries genuine emotional weight, which is a real reason people buy it,
and it stays in the conversation across decades and creative directors. A favorite
smart-money note from our Dior coverage: a black Lady Dior in vintage often runs a
fraction of current retail.

### 6. Louis Vuitton Neverfull

The Neverfull is the consensus pick that proves the thesis. It has almost no
mythology: it launched in 2007, it is an open tote with a cinch-side "never full"
shape, and that is the story (sourced to Yoogi's Closet, PurseBlog, and Vogue). What it
has instead is recognition at a scale very few luxury bags reach. It is the bag that
made the everyday-luxury tote a category, the one you can spot in any city in the world,
and the entry point into the whole conversation for a huge number of people. Low myth,
enormous reach. That is exactly why it belongs next to the Birkin and not several tiers
below it.

## The strong middle (7 to 11)

Less locked, still serious. These are bags with a real claim, where we are confident in
the bag and a little more relaxed about the exact rank.

**7. Fendi Baguette.** The bag that arguably invented the modern "it bag," and the only
one on this list whose nickname entered the language. Silvia Venturini Fendi designed
it in 1997 as a small bag tucked under the arm like a loaf of bread, the deliberate
anti-minimalist answer to the giant bags of the late 90s. It became a cultural object
in a 2000 episode of Sex and the City, when Carrie, robbed at gunpoint, corrects the
mugger: "It's not a bag, it's a Baguette." Fendi discontinued it in the 2000s and
revived it in 2019 (sourced to Fashionphile's Herstory, Luxury London, and Harper's
Bazaar). For sheer cultural fingerprint, it punches well above its rank.

**8. Gucci Jackie.** A genuine heritage-core piece. Christie's groups it with the Bamboo
and the Horsebit as the "Gucci Trinity," the collector's center of the house. It dates
to 1961, was originally called the "Fifties Constance," and was renamed for Jackie
Kennedy after she was repeatedly photographed with it (sourced to Christie's and Vogue).
The crescent shape and piston closure are the tells. It earns its place on heritage and
on a name that did the cultural work for it.

**9. Céline Luggage.** The bag that defined an entire aesthetic era. Phoebe Philo's first
"it bag," introduced Spring 2010, with the distinctive "winged" sides and a front zip
that reads almost like a face (sourced to Spotted Fashion and Weekly Lux Drop). It was
discontinued in March 2025, which only sharpened its standing: "Old Céline" is now one of
the strongest quiet-luxury resale stories going. Recognition among people who care, which
is its own tier of recognition.

**10. Chanel Boy.** The Classic Flap's tougher, more modern counterpart: a chunkier
chain, a heavier rectangular hardware clasp, a deliberately more androgynous line. It
gave Chanel a second pillar bag for buyers who find the Flap too delicate, and it has
held a permanent place in the lineup since its introduction in the early 2010s. We are
keeping the exact launch year off the page pending a clean source. It earns the middle
on real, lasting recognition rather than a single origin myth.

**11. Hermès Constance.** The quiet third Hermès, beloved by people who already own the
other two. The oversized H clasp is the whole identity, clean and immediately legible.
It does not have the Birkin's mass mythology, and we are not going to assign it a
founding date we cannot source, but among collectors its standing is not really in
question. A connoisseur's pick that keeps showing up on best-ever lists for a reason.

## Where even we debate it (12 to 20)

This is the fun part, and we are leaning into it rather than apologizing for it. Fashion
is subjective; we do not judge. The further down you go, the more these are arguments we
are still having, and your list would reasonably look different. A line each, and the
floor is yours.

**12. Gucci GG Marmont.** The 2010s "it bag" of the social-media era: the antique-gold
double-G, the chevron quilting, recognized instantly by an entire generation. It launched
in the mid-2010s (often cited as 2016, a date we are treating as approximate). Argue its
rank, not its reach.

**13. Saint Laurent Loulou (or the Cassandre, broadly).** The matelassé-chevron shoulder
bag with the gleaming Y-S-L clasp, the 1963 Cassandre monogram cast in metal. It is the
YSL most people picture, and a strong case for any "modern classic" list.

**14. Bottega Veneta Jodie.** The no-logo house's breakout: a soft knotted hobo named
after a paparazzi shot of Jodie Foster, all Intrecciato woven leather and no hardware
shouting. The case for it is the whole quiet-luxury turn.

**15. Dior Saddle.** The Y2K bag that came all the way back. Pure of-its-moment design,
then a full second life, which is its own argument for staying power.

**16. Prada Galleria (and the nylon Re-Edition).** Two Pradas, really: the Saffiano
Galleria (the patented cross-hatch leather goes back to 1913) and the nylon Re-Edition
bags reviving the 90s and early-2000s archive. Both wear the "dal 1913" triangle. Endlessly
debatable which one belongs here.

**17. Loewe Puzzle.** Jonathan Anderson's first Loewe bag, spring 2015, the origami-folded
geometry that announced the house's modern era. A designer's-designer pick.

**18. Balenciaga City.** The 2000s moto bag with the whipstitch and tassel zips that
defined indie-sleaze cool. A real "it bag" for its decade, and a fair fight over whether
that earns all-time status. [No bag page yet, no CTA until our data pull lands.]

**19. Mulberry Bayswater.** Britain's everyday icon: the postman's-lock flap, the
sensible-luxury staple a whole country recognizes on sight. Strong regional case,
contested globally. [No bag page yet, no CTA until our data pull lands.]

**20. Telfar.** The newest entry and the most debated, on purpose. The "Bushwick Birkin,"
a genuinely democratic it bag built on access rather than scarcity, the inverse of how
most of this list got famous. Whether a bag this new makes an all-time list is exactly
the kind of argument this section is for. [No bag page yet, no CTA until our data pull
lands.]

So: two axes, one list, and a lot of room to disagree past the top two. That is not a
weakness in the ranking. It is what an "it bag of all time" actually is, a thing the
culture keeps negotiating. Tell us where we got it wrong.

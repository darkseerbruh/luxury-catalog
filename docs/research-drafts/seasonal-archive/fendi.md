# Fendi: seasonal naming archive (models + materials/motifs + named colors)

*Archivist run, 2026-06-28 (house 9, "other houses" queue, after Celine). Fendi is a Regime A
house: it assigns official model names, so the model name IS the canonical key. Three layers
below: a Models table (with debut year + designer where sourced), a Materials/signature-motifs
table led by the FF Zucca logo canvas and the Selleria leather line, and a Named-colors section.
Every row carries a source URL and a confidence. Companion data file: `fendi.jsonl`.*

## The single most important thing about Fendi naming: the BAGS are Silvia's, the FUR/RTW was Karl's (read first)

The load-bearing attribution fact for Fendi, and the one most often gotten wrong, is **who designed
the bags**:

1. **Silvia Venturini Fendi is the house bag designer.** A third-generation Fendi (daughter of Anna,
   granddaughter of founders Adele + Edoardo), she was made Head of Accessories and Menswear in 1994
   and **created the Baguette in 1997** and the **Peekaboo** (debut ~2008/2009). She also designed
   the **Spy** (2005). Do NOT over-credit Lagerfeld for the bags. Sourced: Fashionphile "Silvia
   Venturini Fendi's Herstory," PurseBop, Harper's Bazaar.
2. **Karl Lagerfeld led fur + ready-to-wear, 1965-2019** (a 54-year tenure, the longest in fashion).
   His bag-relevant contribution is the **logos**: the FF "Zucca" double-F (1965/1966, "Fun Fur") and
   the **Karligraphy** monogram (1981). He designed the marks the bags wear, not the bag silhouettes.
3. **Kim Jones led womenswear + couture, 2020-2024.** His bag canon: the **Fendi First** (debut
   Fall 2021, F-shaped clasp) and the **Fendigraphy** (2022). The Peekaboo ISeeU (Fall 2020) and
   Sunshine Shopper were reimagined under his tenure. Silvia stayed on for accessories/menswear
   throughout.
4. **2025-2026 update (flag this, it post-dates the brief):** following Fendi's 2025 centenary,
   **Silvia Venturini Fendi moved to Honorary President on October 1, 2025** (staying at the helm of
   menswear + accessories), and **Maria Grazia Chiuri joined as Creative Director of Womenswear and
   Haute Couture**, with her debut runway show in **February 2026** paying tribute to Silvia's icons
   (new Baguette / Spy / Peekaboo). Chiuri had worked under Silvia at Fendi in the early 1990s.
   Sourced: Fashionphile Herstory (published 2026), Vogue (Chiuri Fendi debut). So the Kim-Jones-leads
   line in the brief was correct for 2020-2024; Chiuri is the current womenswear/couture lead.

So Fendi's naming weight sits on **the model (Silvia's bags)** and **the logo/material (Karl's FF +
the Selleria leather line)**, not on a per-season color lexicon (see §3).

## The cultural moment: the Baguette and Sex and the City (the heritage hook)

The Baguette is the house's GEO-and-engagement crown jewel because of its **Sex and the City** moment:
in a 2000 episode, Carrie Bradshaw is robbed at gunpoint and, when the mugger calls it "a bag," she
corrects him: **"It's not a bag, it's a Baguette!"** That scene is widely credited with making the
Baguette the original "It bag." The Baguette went on hiatus (production discontinued in the 2000s),
was **revived in 2019**, and Fendi opened New York Fashion Week in **September 2022 for the Baguette's
25th anniversary**. To date there have been **more than 1,000 Baguette variations**. Sourced:
Fashionphile Herstory, Luxury London buyer's guide, Harper's Bazaar.

## The honest finding on colors (the same as Dior, Gucci, YSL, and Celine, NOT Hermès/Bottega)

**Fendi does NOT publish a unique per-season color-name dictionary.** Like Dior, Gucci, Saint Laurent,
and Celine (and unlike Hermès and Bottega Veneta), Fendi leans on its **logo canvases** (FF Zucca,
Zucchino, FF 1974) and **plain neutral descriptors** ("Brown," "White," "camelia-colored calfskin")
rather than a poetic seasonal-color lexicon. The differentiating "name" on a Fendi bag is the
**model** + the **material/logo treatment** (Zucca canvas vs Selleria leather vs FF 1974 embossed),
far more than a color name. Fendi.com product copy names colors as flat descriptors. So this file's
color layer is honest and short, and says plainly where a named seasonal color does not exist rather
than inventing one. This puts Fendi firmly in the "does not name its colors" camp.

## How to read confidence

- **high** = house/official fact (fendi.com copy, an official appointment date, official Fendi social
  post), or a fact cross-checked across two independent strong references.
- **medium** = single well-sourced reference site / reseller blog, consistent with the beat but not
  double-confirmed this run.
- **low** = single mention or established-beat knowledge not re-sourced this run; a lead to verify.

I did NOT invent a single model, motif, color, or year. Where a debut year was not sourced this run,
the row says so (null + "not sourced") rather than carrying a fabricated year. Two briefed models
(Kan I / Kan U, Touch) did not surface with a clean debut year and are logged honestly as
beat-knowledge / unsourced-year leads, not fabricated.

### The Zucca vs Zucchino distinction (the GEO-valuable decoder)

This is the detail resellers and buyers most often confuse, and the one worth owning:

- **Zucca** = the **larger** interlocking FF logo (the famous "inverted Zucca," the noughties-era
  logomania print). This is the default "FF monogram canvas."
- **Zucchino** = the **smaller-scale** FF, a finer jacquard "mini Zucca," **discontinued** and now a
  vintage-collector pattern.
- **FF 1974** = the **modern embossed** double-F (the Zucca rendered in 3D on lambskin), the current
  treatment.

All three are the same double-F mark at different scales/finishes. The "FF" itself stands for **"Fun
Fur,"** Lagerfeld's joke about the fur department he was hired to modernize. (Source: fashionphile
"Iconic Fendi Logos.")

*Note: the Zucca (FF) is to Fendi what the Cassandre is to YSL, the Triomphe to Celine, or the
Intrecciato to Bottega — the load-bearing "name" a resale listing turns on. But unlike those, Fendi's
DNA is split across TWO signatures: the **logo** (Karl's FF) and the **leather craft** (the Selleria
saddle-stitch, the house's actual 1925 origin). A Fendi listing is identified by model + which of
those two signatures it carries.*

## 3. Named colors / signatures (the honest, short layer)

**Fendi does not publish a unique per-season color-name dictionary** (the Dior / Gucci / YSL / Celine
pattern, not the Hermès / Bottega one). The bag is identified by **model + logo/material treatment**
(Zucca canvas vs Selleria leather vs FF 1974), and the colors are plain descriptors. So this layer
captures the descriptor convention Fendi actually uses. Nothing here is invented; where a seasonal
color name does not exist I say so.

### What does NOT exist (stated plainly, not invented)

- **No per-season Fendi color-name lexicon.** Unlike Hermès (Étoupe 18) or Bottega (Parakeet,
  Fondant), Fendi does not name its seasonal colors. The naming weight is on the **logo canvas** (FF
  Zucca, Zucchino, FF 1974) and the **leather line** (Selleria), with colors as plain descriptors
  (Brown, Black, Camelia). Do NOT log invented Fendi season colors. The fendi.com color-name field is
  a flat shade name (e.g. "White" for camelia calfskin), never a season-coded poetic name.

## Cross-house context: where Fendi sits on the "names its colors" axis

This run adds Fendi to the sourced cross-house contrast (see `bottega-veneta.md` / `celine.md` for the
full frame):

- **NAME their per-season colors** (a real named-color lexicon exists): **Hermès** (Étoupe 18, Rouge
  H 46…), **Bottega Veneta** (Parakeet, Fondant, Porridge…).
- **Do NOT name most per-season colors** (plain descriptors, no house lexicon): **Dior**, **Gucci**
  (Rosso Ancora the lone exception), **Chanel** (style code + season code), **Saint Laurent**,
  **Celine**, and now **Fendi** — identified by **model + logo/material treatment** (FF Zucca canvas,
  Selleria leather), with neutrals (Brown, Black, Camelia) as plain descriptors.

So Fendi is firmly in the Dior / Gucci / YSL / Celine camp. Its distinctiveness is the **two-signature
DNA** (Karl's FF logo + the 1925 Selleria saddle-stitch leather craft) and the **Silvia-vs-Karl
attribution** (the bags are Silvia's; the logos and fur/RTW are Karl's) — a model-and-material naming
regime, not a color-naming one.

## Cultural-layer read (dated 2026-06-28, hedged)

- **The Baguette is the live heritage-and-revival lane, powered by Sex and the City.** The "It's a
  Baguette!" SATC scene is still the single richest cultural reference Fendi owns, and the 2019 revival
  plus the 2025 Mamma Baguette keep it the bag the conversation centers on. My read, not a house
  statement: the Baguette + SATC is one of the strongest "origin-of-the-It-bag" narratives in luxury,
  and it is evergreen content rather than a fading trend.
- **The Peekaboo is the steady quiet-luxury core; the First is the modern logo play.** The Peekaboo
  (Silvia's anti-logomania bag) and its ISeeU evolution are the everyday icon; the Fendi First (Kim
  Jones, F-clasp) is the photogenic, logo-forward modern flagship. My take, not confirmed by the house:
  among current Fendi bags the Baguette, Peekaboo, and First are the three the market is built on.
- **The womenswear/couture leadership is the live unknown.** Kim Jones left in 2024; Maria Grazia
  Chiuri took over womenswear + couture (debut Feb 2026) with Silvia as Honorary President. Chiuri's
  new bag names (if any) are the live thing to watch — so far her Feb 2026 debut remixed Silvia's
  icons (Baguette, Spy, Peekaboo) rather than launching a clean-slate canon. The safe long-term keys
  are the dated heritage models (Baguette 1997, Peekaboo ~2008/2009, Selleria 1925), not the
  per-director names.

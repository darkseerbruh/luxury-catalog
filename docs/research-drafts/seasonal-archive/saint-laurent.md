# Saint Laurent (YSL): seasonal naming archive (models + materials/motifs + named colors)

*Archivist run, 2026-06-28 (house 7, "other houses" queue, after Bottega Veneta). Saint Laurent
is a Regime A house: it assigns official model names, so the model name IS the canonical key.
Three layers below: a Models table (with designer era), a Materials/signature-motifs table led by
the Cassandre monogram and matelassé quilting, and a Named-colors section. Every row carries a
source URL and a confidence. Companion data file: `saint-laurent.jsonl`.*

## The two things that make YSL naming distinctive (read first)

1. **The YSL → Saint Laurent rebrand under Hedi Slimane (2012).** When Slimane took over in 2012 he
   dropped "Yves" from the ready-to-wear logo, rebranding the house **Saint Laurent** (the legal
   entity stayed Yves Saint Laurent, and the **YSL/Cassandre monogram emblem itself was kept**). So
   "Yves Saint Laurent" pieces are typically pre-2012 (Ford/Pilati era) and "Saint Laurent" pieces
   are Slimane-onward, but the YSL monogram appears on bags from both eras. Sourced: Rebag history,
   Who What Wear.
2. **Designer era is load-bearing for YSL dating.** Tom Ford (1999-2004, accessories rebrand +
   Mombasa/Downtown era), Stefano Pilati (2004-2012, the Muse era), Hedi Slimane (2012-2016, Sac de
   Jour/Sunset + the rebrand), Anthony Vaccarello (2016-present, Niki/Loulou-resurgence/Cassandra/
   Le 5 à 7/Kaia/Manhattan-revival). Most modern It-bags map cleanly to one era; each is attributed
   where sourced.

## The honest finding on colors (the same as Dior and Gucci, NOT Bottega)

**YSL does NOT publish a unique per-season color name dictionary for most bags.** Like Dior and
Gucci (and unlike Hermès and Bottega Veneta), Saint Laurent leans on **neutrals + hardware tone**
(gold vs silver Cassandre) and plain descriptive color names (Noir, Blanc/Crème, Rouge, Dark Beige,
Storm) rather than a named seasonal-color lexicon. The differentiating "name" on a YSL bag is far
more often the **material** (Grain de Poudre, matelassé Chevron) and the **hardware finish** than a
poetic color name. So this file's color layer is honest and short: I capture the signature
darks/neutrals and the hardware-tone axis, and I say plainly where a named seasonal color does not
exist rather than inventing one. This puts YSL in the "does not name its colors" camp with Dior/Gucci.

## How to read confidence

- **high** = house/official fact (an official YSL campaign tag dating a bag), an auction/press fact,
  or a fact cross-checked across two independent strong references.
- **medium** = single well-sourced reference site / reseller blog, consistent with the beat but not
  double-confirmed this run.
- **low** = single mention or established-beat knowledge not re-sourced this run; a lead to verify.

I did NOT invent a single model, motif, color, or year. Where a debut year was not sourced this run,
the row says so (null + "not sourced") rather than carrying a fabricated year.

## 3. Named colors / signatures (the honest, short layer)

**YSL does not publish a unique per-season color-name dictionary** (the Dior/Gucci pattern, not the
Hermès/Bottega one). The bag is identified by model + material + **hardware tone**, and the colors
are plain descriptors. So this layer captures (a) the signature darks/neutrals YSL actually leans
on, (b) the hardware-tone axis that does carry naming weight, and (c) a couple of genuinely-named
finishes I could source. Nothing here is invented; where a seasonal color name does not exist I say so.

*Honest scope note: I logged **no invented seasonal color names.** Saint Laurent's per-season color
on the Loulou/Kate/Sac de Jour is a plain descriptor (Noir, Crème, Dark Beige, Rouge), not a house
lexicon entry. The naming weight in the YSL resale vocabulary sits on the **material** (Grain de
Poudre, matelassé chevron) and the **hardware tone** (gold vs silver Cassandre), which is why those
are the rows with high confidence and the color rows are plain. This is the same finding as Dior and
Gucci, and the opposite of Bottega Veneta and Hermès.*

## Cross-house context: where YSL sits on the "names its colors" axis

This run adds Saint Laurent to the sourced cross-house contrast (see `bottega-veneta.md` for the
full frame):

- **NAME their per-season colors** (a real named-color lexicon exists): **Hermès** (Étoupe 18, Rouge
  H 46…), **Bottega Veneta** (Parakeet, Fondant, Porridge…).
- **Do NOT name most per-season colors** (plain descriptors, no house lexicon): **Dior**, **Gucci**
  (Rosso Ancora is the lone named exception), **Chanel** (style code + season code, color unnamed),
  and now **Saint Laurent** — which instead front-loads the **material name** (Grain de Poudre) and
  the **hardware tone** (gold/silver Cassandre) as the differentiators.

So YSL is firmly in the Dior/Gucci camp. Its distinctiveness is the **Cassandre monogram (1963)** as
the canonical emblem and the **chevron matelassé** as its quilt signature — a material-and-emblem
naming regime, not a color-naming one.

## Cultural-layer read (dated 2026-06-28, hedged)

- **The Mombasa is having a sourced revival.** Tom Ford's 2002 horn-handled hobo was relaunched by
  the house and fronted by **Bella Hadid** (the "Mombasa Small in Leather" is current at YSL.com);
  the BV Sardine (Blazy, FW2022) is widely read as a Mombasa homage, which itself drove renewed
  Mombasa interest. My read, not a house statement: the Y2K-YSL archive (Mombasa, Muse, Downtown,
  Muse Two) is the live revival lane, and the horn-handle Mombasa is the flagship of it.
- **The live everyday-It-bag is the Le 5 à 7.** Reseller and IG chatter centers it as the modern
  "perfect little black bag" (Hailey Bieber spotted repeatedly). My take: among current Vaccarello
  bags, the Le 5 à 7 and the Loulou are the two the conversation is built on, with the Cassandra and
  Icare as the structured/oversized alternatives. Opinion, not a house statement.
- **Vaccarello is the stable era.** Unlike Bottega (three CDs in six years) or Dior/Gucci (recent
  changes), Vaccarello has held Saint Laurent since 2016, so the modern model names (Loulou, Niki,
  Sunset, Cassandra, Kaia, Le 5 à 7, Icare) are stable, well-established keys — safe to catalog.

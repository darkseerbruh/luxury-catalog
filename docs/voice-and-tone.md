# Voice & Tone — Brand Styleguide

*Created 2026-06-21. Updated 2026-06-22. The canonical guide to how Luxury Catalog
sounds — in social, email, on-page editorial, and premium-tool UI. Written to be
applied by a human and by AI-assisted drafting alike. Pairs with
`docs/social-content-calendar.md` (where this voice gets used) and
`docs/marketing-plan.md` (why these channels).*

> **The job of this doc:** make the voice reproducible. Good copy already happens
> here intuitively; undocumented, it can't be handed to a freelancer, a future
> hire, or an AI draft without drifting. This is the spec that keeps every surface
> sounding like one person who *actually knows bags* and gives it to you straight.

---

## 0. The opportunity — a fresh voice, led by what we know

Most secondhand-luxury brands reach for one of two registers: **heritage-house
hush** (serif type, "timeless elegance," reverent distance) or **discount
breathlessness** ("SCORE designer for LESS!!"). Both are wrong for us.

Luxury Catalog is the **IMDb/Fragrantica for bags** — a fact-dense reference asset
(see `docs/engagement-strategy.md` §0). Our readers arrive *mid-decision*, holding
a bag, deciding whether to spend or whether they got a steal. So the voice is built
on one thing first: **we actually know.** Deeply informed, precise, and — this is
the part nobody else gets right — *warm while being that informed.* Rigor is the
mission. The warmth is how we deliver it.

The strategic frame from the marketing plan reinforces it: our defensible channel
is being the **source AI and Google cite** — which rewards clarity, specificity,
and fact density over ornament (`docs/marketing-plan.md` Decision 2). A precise,
plain-spoken voice isn't just on-brand; it's *literally the SEO/GEO strategy
expressed as prose.*

### The two audiences we speak to in one voice

| | The flipper / discoverer | The collector / investor |
|---|---|---|
| Who | 19-year-old at Goodwill, Coach thrift-flipper, "is this real / will it sell" | Tracks a Birkin as an asset, premium-tool buyer, "is this holding value" |
| Mindset | Hunt, hustle, taste-as-status, fast | Patience, provenance, value-over-time |
| What they need | Quick confidence, the flex, the resale angle | Rigor, precision, numbers they can trust |

These look like two brands. They're **one voice flexing its register** (see §4).
The shared ground is the thing both people actually are: *someone who loves the
craft, wants to win at it, and wants to be smart with their money.* Speak to that
and you don't need two voices.

## 1. The ethos block — our north star

> **Manifesto:**
> *Knowing a bag — what's real, what it's worth, where to buy it smart, and whether
> it's worth it to* you *— used to take the right sales associate, the right forum,
> or an expensive mistake. We hand you all of it straight: deeply informed, plainly
> told, no snobbery. We're the friend you call when you've found something you
> love — who knows enough to tell you the truth, and, when it's right, to say: that
> was a great choice, and totally worth it.*

> **Taglines:**
> *Carry a little art. Choose it well.*
> *Know what it's worth — and what it's worth to you.*

> **Voice in one line:**
> *A friend who actually knows bags — deeply informed, warm, a little obsessed —
> who gives it to you straight and, when it's right, tells you it was absolutely
> worth it.*

### The honest "why" underneath
Sustainability is the polite answer to "why buy secondhand luxury," and on its own
it's limp. The honest answer is a mix — and naming it plainly is what makes a
reader feel *seen* instead of *sold to*: **money** (getting it for less, or making
it on the flip), **taste** (the pleasure of a good eye), **craft** (genuine love
of the object), and **belonging** (wanting to be someone who knows). We name those
honestly and let the reader into the club — not with fake exclusivity, but with
real knowledge handed over. The feeling we're after: *"finally, someone who talks
about this the way I think about it, and just told me the thing the gatekeepers
wouldn't."*

## 2. What we learn from the references

We **learn from these creators — we don't impersonate them.** Each isolates a
technique we want in the voice.

### @jesuislou — the primary tonal model
Lou (Barcelona, handbag YouTuber) is the closest existing voice to where we're
going: **deeply informed, genuinely warm, allergic to snobbery.** From her actual
videos:

- **Coffee-with-a-friend intimacy.** She opens by inviting you in — *"pause this
  video, grab your coffee, your tea, whatever you're drinking, a couple of snacks,
  and let's just talk about bags."* Confessional, unscripted, always second person.
  **Steal:** talk *with* the reader, not at them. (Great default for the newsletter
  open — see `docs/social-content-calendar.md`.)
- **Transparency as mission.** On sharing the Birkin price: *"not for bragging —
  there's nothing on the internet about Hermès pricing… I just want to be
  transparent."* **This is literally our reason to exist** — handing over the
  premium information that's been gatekept. Elevate it everywhere.
- **Earned precision.** She gushes, but it's always pinned to fact: she's 5'1" so
  the 25 suits her frame; Togo vs. Epsom vs. lambskin vs. caviar; *"the 25 fits
  everything I need daily, not an iPad or a laptop"*; wear-tested — *"I already
  dropped it, spilled coffee on it, and it was perfect."* **Steal:** the rigor is
  what makes the warmth credible. Never gush without a concrete reason attached.
- **Smart-with-your-money, woven in.** Pre-loved sources, candid prices, *"go
  vintage for a black Lady Dior and pay a fraction of retail,"* *"the 35 is
  well-priced on resale — you skip all the hoops of the store."* **Steal:** she's
  the proof our two audiences are one person — emotional collector *and* shrewd
  secondhand shopper.
- **Permission-giving, never judging.** *"We listen and we don't judge,"* *"you do
  you,"* *"fashion is subjective… that's the only thing that matters."* **Steal:**
  no shame — not about budget, not about why you buy, not about how many.
- **Buy-what-you-love over trends.** *"If I love it, I'll still buy it";* she waits
  trends out *"to make sure I'm not being influenced by social media."* **Steal:**
  grounded, classic-over-hype counsel.
- **Reassurance at the moment of joy.** *"This is me in a bag,"* *"it makes me
  happy,"* *"that was a great choice."* **Steal:** be the friend who validates a
  great call. (More on the persona/system split in §5 — Lou's first-person *"love
  love love"* warmth belongs to the founder-persona, not the brand's sales voice.)

### Secondary models — single techniques
- **@relaxitsonlyfashion** — *deadpan delivery, real expertise underneath.*
  Authority from obvious deep knowledge, never from raising the voice. Steal:
  confidence without hype.
- **@AlexandraAnele** — *approachable + technically exacting in the same breath.*
  "Here's the real thing nobody tells you," said like a friend, backed like an
  expert.
- **@juliareingoldproductions** — *human-on-the-other-side.* The model for our
  founder-as-face persona (§5): warm, present, respects the viewer's intelligence.

### The brand-voice canon (operating rules)
- **Mr Porter** — *"Would you say it in the pub?"* The test for every line.
- **Mailchimp (minus the quirk)** — *"More important to be clear than entertaining."*
- **Aesop** — precision; **no empty superlatives.** Specifics over adjectives.
- **Ffern** — *make the process and mechanics feel human.* Name the thing, the
  place, the season; let the reader feel the hand behind it.

## 3. The five core principles

Ordered by weight. Rigor leads; joy is the air the whole thing breathes.

### Principle 1 — Deeply informed, warmly told *(the mission)*
We actually know, and we prove it — then we say it like a human. Precision is the
product; warmth is the delivery. Not "warm with facts attached" — *informed to the
point that the warmth is earned.*
- **Do:** "The 25 fits everything for a normal day — phone, cardholder, sunglasses
  — just not a laptop. At 5'1" it's the size that doesn't wear you."
- **Don't:** "It's a gorgeous, versatile size that works for everyone!" (knows
  nothing, says nothing)
- *Why:* it's also the GEO strategy — specific, fact-dense pages get cited.

### Principle 2 — Clear over clever
Borrowed from Mailchimp: if a sentence makes the reader decode it, it failed, no
matter how good the joke was. Lead with the answer.
- **Do:** "Two interior zip pockets means it's fake — or pre-2008."
- **Don't:** "There's an interesting little tell hiding in the lining that the
  cognoscenti know to look for…"

### Principle 3 — Be the friend at the moment of joy
We're who you call when you've found something you love. We know enough to tell you
the truth, and when it's right, to say *that was a great choice.* This is the warmth
under everything — reassurance, validation, taste-as-identity ("this is me in a
bag"). Joy is the value, not just economics (the "smiles per mile").
- **Do:** "Honestly? Buy it. The caramel lambskin is the one you'll actually reach
  for, it's holding value, and you've wanted it for two years. That's a great call."
- **Don't:** "This purchase aligns with current market resale trends." (correct;
  cold; not a friend)

### Principle 4 — Smart with your money *(the practical why)*
Being savvy is a real reason people use us, and we respect it openly. Where to buy
smart, what holds value, when vintage or pre-loved beats retail, what it actually
resells for. Never shame the money motive — name it.
- **Do:** "Skip the boutique waitlist — a 35 in great condition runs well under
  retail on resale, and you get the exact color you want."
- **Don't:** pretend price is gauche. It's why they're here.

### Principle 5 — No snobbery, no gatekeeping *(the guardrail)*
This one is a *check*, not a headline. We don't lead with "a bag isn't status" —
that's defensive. But we never gatekeep, never make anyone feel dumb for asking,
never flex. When status-anxiety is the elephant in the room, we name it plainly and
move on.
- **Do:** "'Blind stamp' just means the date/maker stamp pressed into the leather
  without ink. Here's where Hermès puts it."
- **Don't:** "As any serious collector knows, the blind stamp is elementary."

## 4. One voice, flexing by surface

We do **not** run two voices for two audiences. One voice **flexes register** —
warmer and looser where people are discovering, tighter and more exacting where
money is on the line. The constant: *someone who actually knows and gives it to you
straight.*

### The register dial

| Surface | Register | Looser ↔ Tighter | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| TikTok / Shorts hook | Loosest — fast, warm, deadpan | ◀━━━━━○ | Discovery; earn the watch in 2 seconds |
| Pinterest pin | Loose but useful — visual + one real fact | ◀━━━○━ | Search-driven, long half-life |
| Instagram caption | Mid — warm, a little wit, one takeaway | ━━○━━ | Relationship-building |
| Newsletter | Mid — coffee-with-a-friend; informed | ━━○━━ | Owned audience; the "let's talk bags" register |
| On-page editorial / GEO answer | Tighter — clear, fact-dense, sourced | ━○━━━ | Cited by AI; mid-decision readers |
| Authentication markers | Tightest — exact, hedged where uncertain | ○━━━━ | Wrong call = real harm + brand risk |
| Price / value & premium-tool UI | Tightest — precise numbers, no hype | ○━━━━ | The money-moment; trust is everything |

**The flex is in register, never in identity.** A TikTok hook and a price-history
caption are recognizably the same person — one's cracking a joke at Goodwill, the
other's reading you the comps straight. The voice gets *more precise* as stakes
rise, never more pompous.

> **Same fact, flexed:**
> - *TikTok:* "POV: the Goodwill Coach is real and you're about to make $200."
> - *Pin:* "Real vs. fake Coach Tabby: start with the turn-lock weight."
> - *Newsletter:* "The Tabby keeps turning up secondhand under $200 — here's how to
>   spot the real ones, and what they're actually reselling for."
> - *Price tool:* "Tabby 26, good condition: median resale $180, up 6% this quarter."

## 5. Founder-persona vs. brand-system

Keep these two separate so the brand scales beyond one person.

- **The brand voice (this doc)** — the *transferable system*. Applied by any writer
  or AI on any surface. Deeply informed, warmly told, no snobbery. This is what
  makes the brand reproducible.
- **The founder-as-face persona** — for talking-head video (the @juliareingold
  model; the marketing plan's "be the face," Decision 5). Here the founder can be
  **as personal as she likes** — her real opinions, her own thrift finds, her
  camera presence.

**The "stunning" rule (important).** Lou says *"stunning"* and *"love love love"*
constantly, and it works — because it's **first-person**, **always pinned to a
concrete reason**, and **never used to sell you.** That latitude belongs to the
**founder-persona**: on camera, first-person gush is genuine and on-brand. The
**brand system** stays disciplined — it earns enthusiasm through *specificity*, not
empty superlatives in a sales voice. Same warmth, two settings: *"I am obsessed
with this caramel lambskin"* (founder, fine) vs. *"the most stunning must-have
bag!"* (brand sales voice, never — see §7).

The relationship: the persona is the brand voice **with a face and a first person.**
The website, email, and faceless social run on the brand voice alone, so the
business never depends on the founder filming forever.

## 6. Bridging the two audiences (in one voice)

Same voice, dialed by register — not a separate brand for Gen Z vs. collectors.

- **To the 19-year-old flipper:** looser end. Lead with the hunt, the flex, the
  resale upside. Stay precise on the part that costs them money. *"Goodwill Coach
  for $12 that resells at $180 — if the creed stamp checks out. Here's the
  10-second check."*
- **To the collector tracking an asset:** tighter end. Lead with rigor and
  provenance. Keep the warmth and the no-gatekeeping — they hate condescension as
  much as anyone. *"The 2024 Kelly 25 in Togo is holding value better than the
  Birkin 30 this year — here's the resale spread and why."*
- **The bridge sentence test:** a good line lands for both because it's *true,
  specific, unpretentious, and a little warm.* "Know what it's worth — and what
  it's worth to you" works at Goodwill and at the auction preview. Write to the
  shared self (loves the craft, wants to win, wants to be smart) and the register
  handles the rest.

## 7. Word lists

### Words & moves we use
- Plain verbs: *check, spot, tell, find, flip, carry, live with, hold value, source.*
- Concrete nouns: *creed stamp, blind stamp, turn-lock, caviar leather, date code,
  comps, spread.*
- Honest money talk: *resale, comps, holding value, what it actually sells for,
  steal, flip, a fraction of retail.*
- Joy / reassurance: *a little art, worth it to you, totally worth it, a great
  choice, this is so you.*
- Real specifics: years, cm **and** inches, prices with ranges, conditions, what
  fits.
- Friend-register connective tissue: *here's the thing, the catch is, start with,
  honestly, if you're asking me, those are my two cents.*

### Words & moves we avoid
- **Hype superlatives** (in brand/sales voice): *stunning, iconic, must-have, to
  die for, obsessed, priceless, ultimate, flawless.* *(The founder-persona gets a
  pass on first-person gush — see §5. The brand voice earns it with specifics.)*
- **Gatekeeping tells:** *as any true collector knows, if you have to ask,
  elevated, curated for the discerning.*
- **Discount-bin shouting:** *SCORE, deal alert, prices SLASHED,* exclamation
  stacks, all-caps urgency.
- **Corporate hedge-speak:** *consumers are advised, please be aware, in order to,
  utilize, leverage (as a verb), at this time.*
- **Flip throwaways that undercut real information:** don't write *"it's just…
  here"* about knowledge that took expertise to assemble. Give it its due weight.
- **Naive declaratives we then overturn (locked 2026-06-30).** Never assert a common
  assumption in our own flat voice as if we hold it, then contradict it (e.g. "A smaller
  bag should cost less."). Attribute the to-be-overturned premise, then turn it: *"you'd
  think…," "the rule says…," "…right?," "common sense says…," "it feels like… it's not."*
  Bald *should / obviously / everyone knows / of course* statements we then reverse tank
  credibility. Attribute, not assert.
- **Movie-trailer cadences:** the portentous three-beat ("Know it. Want it. Then
  decide.") reads cheap. Vary the rhythm.
- **Em dashes (—).** Hard rule: don't use them. They're an AI-slop tell and they
  blur sentence logic. Use a period, comma, colon, or parentheses instead, and
  pick the one that fits the thought:
  - aside or pause → comma ("Photograph any bag, and we check it against the markers")
  - hard stop / two ideas → period or semicolon ("Track what you own. Watch it grow.")
  - setup → payoff → colon ("What it's worth: The real resale range, dated and sourced.")
  *(The one deliberate exception is the tagline "Know what it's worth — and what
  it's worth to you," which keeps its dash by owner decision. Everything else obeys
  the rule.)*
- **Capitalize the first word after a colon (locked 2026-06-26).** In any
  user-facing copy, the word right after a colon starts with a capital letter
  ("What it's worth: The real range," "Look up any designer bag: Real prices").
  Applies everywhere copy ships: headings, labels, taglines, metadata, error
  strings. (A label like "Brand: Chanel" already complies since the value is a
  proper noun.)
- **Naive declaratives we don't actually believe (credibility-tanking, locked 2026-06-30).**
  Never assert a common assumption in our own flat voice as if we hold it ("A smaller bag should
  cost less."). We are the knowing narrator, so frame a premise we're about to overturn *as the
  assumption*, not as our belief: "you'd think a smaller bag costs less," "the rule says…," or
  the wink "…right?" Then turn it. A bald "should" we immediately contradict reads as naive and
  tanks trust. Same for any "obviously / everyone knows / of course" we then debunk: attribute
  the belief, don't own it.
- **AI-slop tells:** see §8.

## 7b. Article structure & data rules (locked 2026-06-30, from owner edits)

- **Brand name: "Luxury Catalog," never "The Luxury Catalog."** Sitewide.
- **Open by questioning the reader's default,** not by announcing the finding ("Does a bigger bag
  really cost more?" beats "Smaller bags cost more"). Put a crack in the assumption, then turn it.
- **Social scripts lead with a HOOK and a POINT OF VIEW, never a topic statement (locked 2026-06-30).**
  "Two bags, both cost a used car, and both have a flaw nobody admits" beats "Birkin or Kelly, price
  barely decides it." Inject the owner's real opinion (hedged: "in my experience," "my take") and the
  de-gatekeeping energy. A data summary with no hook reads dry and dies. **Ship 2-3 rotating hook
  variants per post** so she can A/B test and repost without it going stale.
- **Never assume the audience knows model names (locked 2026-06-30).** Casual buyers recognize bags
  by look and brand, not model name (the Coach Tabby is a rare exception, it's TikTok-famous). So
  pair every model name with the bag itself: shown on screen in video, a silhouette/photo in a pin,
  or a 3-word descriptor in copy ("the structured leather satchel, the Mulberry Bayswater"). Lead
  with the names people actually know.
- **Short-form social (TikTok/IG) = ONE idea, one hook, one sticky takeaway (locked 2026-06-30).**
  Never a list or a ranking, nobody remembers seven names and prices in 30 seconds. Lists, rankings,
  and full comparisons are ARTICLE content (and fine on Pinterest, which behaves like search/
  reference). The short-form post extracts ONE beat (ideally a single surprising comparison) and
  routes to the article for the full list. Example: "the trendy Coach is worth a third of another
  Coach from the same brand" beats a 7-bag ranked chart on TikTok.
- **Sample sizes (n) live in charts and tables, never in body prose.** The chart carries the
  receipts; the text stays readable.
- **Multi-point numbers go in scannable bullets,** not a packed sentence.
- **Surface the governing takeaway early** (right after the first example), and give the single
  most important line **visual emphasis** (pull quote / callout), not buried in a paragraph.
- **Hyphenate compound modifiers** (most-wanted, small-size, data-informed).
- **"Should" is fine for practical instructions** ("what you should do with this data"); **banned
  for naive premises we debunk and for taste/value verdicts** (those stay "my take / an estimate";
  see §7 naive-declaratives + §3 hedge).
- **The data-hedge note is explicit and human:** say what the numbers ARE and ARE NOT (asking not
  sold, condition varies, an estimate of the market not an appraisal of one bag), a short readable
  note, not a terse tag.

## 8. AI-assist guardrails (don't let it read like a robot)

We draft with AI (it's the leverage the whole company runs on) but we **never ship
obviously-AI copy** — same principle as the **video hard line**: *AI never generates
footage; AI only edits real footage. Never alter reality (face/voice/appearance/
scene/object), never AI-touch an authentication frame, never strip an AI label*
(full ruleset + TikTok disclosure map: `docs/social-content-calendar.md` §8).
AI-generated tells also get *downweighted by the very AI search engines we're
optimizing for* (`docs/marketing-plan.md` Decision 4), so this is strategy, not just
taste.

### The AI-slop tells to strip every time
- **Hollow tricolons & "not just X but Y":** "It's not just a bag, it's a
  statement." → cut.
- **Empty hype adjectives** doing the work facts should do (see §7).
- **"In today's fast-paced world," "elevate your," "the perfect blend of," "when it
  comes to," "look no further," "dive into," "in conclusion."** → cut on sight.
- **Symmetrical mush:** every sentence the same length and shape. Vary it. Fragments
  are fine.
- **Em dashes (—) at all, and emoji confetti.** Em dashes are banned outright
  (see §7 for the replacements to reach for); one clean idea beats decoration.
- **Vague universality:** "many collectors feel…" with no source. Either cite a real
  number (we have them) or cut it.

### The blacklist (never use these — they read as AI). Added 2026-06-25.
- **Words:** crucial, delve, realm, robust, seamless, unlock, empower, leverage, elevate,
  boasts, nestled, bustling, tapestry, testament, foster, pivotal, myriad, "navigate the
  landscape," "embark on a journey." Reach for the plain word (important, dig into, area,
  strong, smooth) or cut.
- **Filler transitions:** Moreover, Furthermore, Additionally, "That said," "Ultimately,"
  "At the end of the day," "It's worth noting," "It's important to note." Cut them and
  start the sentence.
- **Signposting framings:** "The honest answer is," "Here's what the numbers say," "Here's
  the thing," "Put another way," "Let's break it down," "the whole story," "in plain
  numbers," "the bottom line," "let's face it," "look no further." Just say the thing.
- **The tell is the cluster.** One slips by; three in a paragraph screams AI. Strip them
  all. (Researched against editor write-ups of common ChatGPT tells; pairs with §7's em-dash
  ban and the hype-adjective rule.)

### Human-review checklist (run before publishing any AI-drafted copy)
1. **Informed check** — does this prove we actually know? Is the warmth *earned* by
   a real, specific fact? If it could've been written by someone who's never held
   the bag, rewrite.
2. **Pub test** — would a real person say this out loud to a friend?
3. **Specificity check** — replace adjectives with numbers/facts where we have them.
4. **Never-invent check** — no asserted date code / serial / hardware detail unless
   sourced and high-confidence. Hedge or cut otherwise. *(Non-negotiable — a wrong
   authentication call causes real harm.)*
5. **Slop sweep** — strip the tells above; kill flip throwaways and trailer cadences.
6. **Register check** — does the tightness match the surface (§4 dial)?
7. **One-voice check** — same person as our last three posts? Brand voice, not
   founder-persona gush, unless it's on-camera (§5). **Articles are third person:**
   "the bag people ask about most," never "the bag I get asked about most." First-person
   "I / my" is the founder persona (video only); the brand "we / our" is fine.

> If a draft can't pass 1–7 in under a minute of editing, the draft was wrong —
> rewrite the prompt, don't polish the slop.

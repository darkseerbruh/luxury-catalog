# Voice & Tone — Brand Styleguide

*Created 2026-06-21. The canonical guide to how Luxury Catalog sounds — in
social, email, on-page editorial, and premium-tool UI. Written to be applied by a
human and by AI-assisted drafting alike. Pairs with `docs/social-content-calendar.md`
(where this voice gets used) and `docs/marketing-plan.md` (why these channels).*

> **The job of this doc:** make the voice reproducible. Good copy already happens
> here intuitively; undocumented, it can't be handed to a freelancer, a future
> hire, or an AI draft without drifting. This is the spec that keeps every surface
> sounding like one person who clearly knows bags and doesn't bullshit.

---

## 0. The opportunity — why a fresh voice, not legacy luxury

Most secondhand-luxury brands reach for one of two registers: **heritage-house
hush** (serif type, "timeless elegance," reverent distance) or **discount
breathlessness** ("SCORE designer for LESS!!"). Both are wrong for us.

Luxury Catalog is the **IMDb/Fragrantica for bags** — a fact-dense reference asset
(see `docs/engagement-strategy.md` §0). Our readers don't arrive for atmosphere;
they arrive *mid-decision*, holding a bag, deciding whether to spend or whether
they got a steal. The voice has to match how those people actually talk and think
about fashion now — which is the **internet-native register**: direct, funny,
genuinely obsessed with the craft, allergic to gatekeeping and to marketing
syrup. That register is the opportunity, and almost nobody in resale owns it.

The strategic frame from the marketing plan reinforces this: our defensible
channel is being the **source AI and Google cite** — which rewards clarity,
specificity, and fact density over ornament (`docs/marketing-plan.md` Decision 2).
A plain-spoken, precise voice isn't just on-brand; it's *literally the SEO/GEO
strategy expressed as prose.*

### The two audiences we speak to in one voice

| | The flipper / discoverer | The collector / investor |
|---|---|---|
| Who | 19-year-old at Goodwill, Coach thrift-flipper, "is this real / will it sell" | Tracks a Birkin as an asset, premium-tool buyer, "is this holding value" |
| Mindset | Hunt, hustle, taste-as-status, fast | Patience, provenance, value-over-time |
| What they need | Quick confidence, the flex, the resale angle | Rigor, precision, numbers they can trust |

These look like two brands. They're **one voice flexing its register** (see §3).
The shared ground is the thing both people actually are: *someone who loves the
craft and wants to win at it.* Speak to that and you don't need two voices.

## 1. Brand ethos — the honest "why"

> **One line:** *Great bags are worth knowing — and you deserve to be in on it.*

Sustainability is the polite answer to "why buy secondhand luxury," and on its own
it's limp — nobody refreshes a resale app for the planet. The honest answer is a
mix, and naming it plainly is what makes a reader feel *seen* instead of *sold to*:

- **Money** — getting it for less, or making money on the flip. Real, not shameful.
- **Taste** — the pleasure of a good eye, of knowing what's good and why.
- **Craft** — genuine love of the object: the leather, the hardware, the history.
- **Belonging / status** — wanting to be someone who *knows*, and be seen knowing.

Our ethos is to **name all four honestly and let the reader into the club.** Not
flatter them with fake exclusivity — *welcome* them with real knowledge. The
feeling we're after: *"finally, someone who talks about this the way I think about
it, and just told me the thing the gatekeepers wouldn't."*

**Recommended ethos lines** (use, adapt, A/B):
1. *Great bags are worth knowing — and you deserve to be in on it.* ← primary
2. *The knowledge used to be gatekept. Now it's just… here.*
3. *Know what it is. Know what it's worth. Then decide.*

This ethos is the foundation every principle below expresses. When a piece of copy
feels off, it's usually because it slipped into selling instead of *letting the
reader in.*

## 2. What we learn from the references

These are the creators the founder loves. We **learn from them — we don't
impersonate them.** Each one isolates a technique we want in the voice.

- **@relaxitsonlyfashion** (Timothy Chernyaev, TikTok) — *deadpan delivery, real
  expertise underneath.* Calm, flat, unhurried; the authority comes from obvious
  deep knowledge (WWD/Vogue/W background, runway recaps), never from raising his
  voice. **Steal:** confidence without hype. Let the facts and the obvious passion
  carry it; don't perform excitement.
- **@AlexandraAnele** (YouTube) — *approachable anti-pretension + technical
  authority.* Titles like *"Dude, blush placement changes your whole face."*
  Friendly, a little blunt, but art-school-rigorous about technique. **Steal:** the
  ability to be casual AND exacting in the same breath — "here's the real thing
  nobody tells you," said like a friend, backed like an expert.
- **@juliareingoldproductions** (TikTok) — *human-on-the-other-side; technology as
  a partner.* The model for our **founder-as-face** persona (see §4): warm,
  present, talks to you not at you. **Steal:** the posture of a real person who
  respects the viewer's intelligence.

Plus the brand-voice canon researched previously:

- **Mr Porter** — *"Would you say it in the pub?"* The test for every line.
- **Mailchimp (minus the quirk)** — *"More important to be clear than entertaining."*
  Plain-spoken, genuine, translator-of-jargon.
- **Aesop** — precision; **no empty superlatives.** Specifics over adjectives.
- **Ffern** — *make the process and mechanics feel human.* Name the thing, the
  place, the season; let the reader feel the hand behind it.

## 3. The voice in one line + five core principles

> **The voice in one line:** *A friend who actually knows bags — funny, exacting,
> and on your side — who refuses to gatekeep and refuses to bullshit.*

### Principle 1 — Clear over clever
Borrowed from Mailchimp: if a sentence makes the reader work to decode it, it
failed, no matter how good the joke was. Lead with the answer.
- **Do:** "Two interior zip pockets means it's fake — or pre-2008."
- **Don't:** "There's an interesting little tell hiding in the lining that the
  cognoscenti know to look for…"
- *Why:* it's also the GEO strategy — front-loaded answers get cited.

### Principle 2 — Passion you can feel, not perform
From @relaxitsonlyfashion. The love of the craft shows through *specificity and
care*, not exclamation points. Dry can be warm when the knowledge is real.
- **Do:** "The caviar leather is the whole point — it's why this one survives a
  decade of daily carry when the lambskin gets corner-rubbed in a year."
- **Don't:** "We are OBSESSED with this stunning iconic must-have!! 😍"

### Principle 3 — Anti-pretension, anti-gatekeeping
From @AlexandraAnele and the ethos. We hand over the knowledge that fashion
usually hoards. Never make the reader feel dumb for not already knowing.
- **Do:** "'Blind stamp' just means the date/maker stamp pressed into the leather
  without ink. Here's where Hermès puts it."
- **Don't:** "As any serious collector knows, the blind stamp is elementary."

### Principle 4 — Reverence, earned authority, precision *(the luxury counterweight)*
This is the pillar that keeps casual from tipping into *cheap*. Luxury here =
**confidence and restraint**, not ornate language. We are reverent about the
*objects and the craft*, exacting at any money-moment, and we never assert what we
can't source (the product's non-negotiable "never invent" rule — see
`docs/handoff.md`). Modern luxury (The Row, Aesop, Mr Porter) already lives in this
restraint, so precision *strengthens* the fresh voice instead of diluting it.
- **Do:** "Retail was about $10,400 in 2024; resale sits roughly $8,000–11,000
  depending on hardware and condition." (specific, sourced, calm)
- **Don't:** "Worth an absolute fortune — basically priceless!" (hype = cheap)
- *Rule of thumb:* the looser the joke, the more precise the facts around it must
  be. Earn the casualness with rigor.

### Principle 5 — Say it like you'd say it to a friend
Mr Porter's pub test + Ffern's human hand. Second person, contractions, real talk.
Name the thing, the place, the season. You're a person texting someone you like,
not a brand addressing a market.
- **Do:** "Found one at Goodwill? Check the creed stamp first — here's how."
- **Don't:** "Consumers are advised to verify authenticity prior to purchase."

## 4. One voice, flexing by surface

We do **not** run two voices for two audiences. We run **one voice that flexes
register** — loose and passion-forward where people are discovering, exacting and
precise where money is on the line. The unifier is constant: *someone who knows
bags and doesn't bullshit.*

### The register dial

| Surface | Register | Looser ↔ Tighter | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| TikTok / Shorts hook | Loosest — fast, funny, deadpan | ◀━━━━━○ | Discovery; earn the watch in 2 seconds |
| Pinterest pin | Loose but useful — visual + one real fact | ◀━━━○━ | Search-driven, long half-life |
| Instagram caption | Mid — warm, a little wit, one takeaway | ━━○━━ | Relationship-building |
| Newsletter | Mid — between "we/you" warm and editorial neutral | ━━○━━ | Owned audience; the "in-between" register |
| On-page editorial / GEO answer | Tighter — clear, fact-dense, sourced | ━○━━━ | Cited by AI; mid-decision readers |
| Authentication markers | Tightest — exact, hedged where uncertain | ○━━━━ | Wrong call = real harm + brand risk |
| Price / value & premium-tool UI | Tightest — precise numbers, no hype | ○━━━━ | This is the money-moment; trust is everything |

**The flex is in register, never in identity.** A TikTok hook and a price-history
caption are recognizably the same person — one's just cracking a joke at Goodwill
and the other's reading you the comps straight. The voice gets *more precise* as
the stakes rise, never more pompous.

> **Worked example of the same fact, flexed:**
> - *TikTok:* "POV: the Goodwill Coach is real and you're about to make $200." 
> - *Pin:* "Real vs. fake Coach Tabby: start with the turn-lock weight."
> - *Newsletter:* "The Tabby keeps showing up secondhand under $200 — here's how
>   to tell the real ones, and what they're actually reselling for."
> - *Price tool:* "Tabby 26, good condition: median resale $180, up 6% this quarter."

## 5. Founder-persona vs. brand-system

Keep these two separate so the brand scales beyond one person.

- **The brand voice (this doc)** — the *transferable system*. Principles abstracted
  from why the founder loves those creators, applicable by any writer or AI on any
  surface. This is what makes the brand reproducible. Everything in §1–§4 is the
  brand voice.
- **The founder-as-face persona** — for talking-head video (the @juliareingold
  model; the marketing plan's "be the face" channel, Decision 5). Here the founder
  can be **as personal and specific as she likes** — her actual opinions, her own
  thrift finds, her camera presence. It's *her*, not a system.

The relationship: the persona is the brand voice **with a face and a first
person.** Same values (clear, passionate, anti-gatekeeping, precise), more personal
latitude. The website, email, and faceless social run on the brand voice alone, so
the business never depends on the founder filming forever (the plan's faceless-engine
principle). When she's on camera, the persona makes it warm; when she isn't, the
system carries it.

## 6. Bridging the two audiences (in one voice)

Same voice, dialed by register — not a separate brand for Gen Z vs. collectors.

- **To the 19-year-old flipper:** lean to the looser end. Lead with the hunt, the
  flex, the resale upside. Stay precise on the part that costs them money (is it
  real, will it sell, for how much). *"Goodwill Coach for $12 that resells at $180 —
  if the creed stamp checks out. Here's the 10-second check."*
- **To the collector tracking an asset:** lean to the tighter end. Lead with rigor
  and provenance. Keep the warmth and the no-gatekeeping — they hate condescension
  as much as anyone. *"The 2024 Kelly 25 in Togo is holding value better than the
  Birkin 30 this year — here's the resale spread and why."*
- **The bridge sentence test:** a good line lands for both because it's *true,
  specific, and unpretentious.* "Know what it is, know what it's worth, then
  decide" works at Goodwill and at the auction preview. Write to the shared self
  (loves the craft, wants to win) and the register handles the rest.

## 7. Word lists

### Words & moves we use
- Plain verbs: *check, spot, tell, find, flip, carry, hold value, source.*
- Concrete nouns: *creed stamp, blind stamp, turn-lock, caviar leather, date code,
  comps, spread.*
- Honest money talk: *resale, comps, holding value, what it actually sells for,
  steal, flip.*
- Real specifics: years, cm **and** inches, prices with ranges, conditions.
- Friend-register connective tissue: *here's the thing, the catch is, start with,
  worth knowing, the tell is.*

### Words & moves we avoid
- **Hype superlatives:** *stunning, iconic, must-have, to die for, obsessed,
  priceless, ultimate, flawless.* (Aesop rule: specifics beat adjectives.)
- **Gatekeeping tells:** *as any true collector knows, if you have to ask,
  elevated, curated for the discerning.*
- **Discount-bin shouting:** *SCORE, deal alert, prices SLASHED,* exclamation
  stacks, all-caps urgency.
- **Corporate hedge-speak:** *consumers are advised, please be aware, in order to,
  utilize, leverage (as a verb), at this time.*
- **AI-slop tells:** see §8.

## 8. AI-assist guardrails (don't let it read like a robot)

We draft with AI (it's the leverage the whole company runs on) but we **never ship
obviously-AI copy** — same principle as the no-obviously-AI-*video* rule
(`docs/handoff.md` constraints). AI-generated tells also get *downweighted by the
very AI search engines we're optimizing for* (`docs/marketing-plan.md` Decision 4),
so this is strategy, not just taste.

### The AI-slop tells to strip every time
- **Hollow tricolons & "not just X but Y":** "It's not just a bag, it's a
  statement." → cut.
- **Empty hype adjectives** doing the work facts should do (see §7).
- **"In today's fast-paced world," "elevate your," "the perfect blend of," "when it
  comes to," "look no further," "dive into," "in conclusion."** → cut on sight.
- **Symmetrical mush:** every sentence the same length and shape. Vary it. Fragments
  are fine.
- **Em-dash overload and emoji confetti.** One clean idea beats decoration.
- **Vague universality:** "many collectors feel…" with no source. Either cite a real
  number (we have them) or cut it.

### Human-review checklist (run before publishing any AI-drafted copy)
1. **Pub test** — would a real person say this out loud to a friend? If not, rewrite.
2. **Specificity check** — is every claim backed by a real fact from the catalog?
   Replace adjectives with numbers where we have them.
3. **Never-invent check** — no asserted date code / serial / hardware detail unless
   it's sourced and confidence is high. Hedge or cut otherwise. *(Non-negotiable —
   a wrong authentication call causes real harm.)*
4. **Slop sweep** — strip the tells above.
5. **Register check** — does the tightness match the surface (§4 dial)?
6. **One-voice check** — does it sound like the same person as our last three posts?

> If a draft can't pass 1–6 in under a minute of editing, the draft was wrong —
> rewrite the prompt, don't polish the slop.

| Bag page intro | "The Chanel Classic Flap is a stunning, iconic must-have that exudes timeless elegance." | "The Chanel Classic Flap has one interior zip pocket. Two means a fake — or a pre-2008 bag. Here's the rest of the date-code story." |
